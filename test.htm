tensile test on polymers

1.     Introduction

The mechanical properties under static load are largely the same for polymers as for metals: modulus of elasticity, yield stress, tensile strength, rupture strain, impact strength, fatigue strength, However, there are a number of notable differences, which will be discussed below.

 

As with metals, a simple tensile test is performed to determine some of these properties for different polymers.

 

 

2.     Theory

When preparing for this experiment, first read the theory part of the lab text "Tensile test on metals" (paragraph 2), and possibly the relevant chapters in the course text! There a number of theoretical concepts are clarified. The rest of this section is supplementary to this that is specific to polymers.

 

2.1.        Classification on the basis of mechanical behaviour

Polymers generally consist of long chain-shaped molecules, with or without side branches, that can be chemically bound to each other in various ways. The length of the chains can vary greatly. In addition, the molecules may be more or less "folded" according to an ordered pattern, causing the crystalline structure to vary from amorphous to crystalline. This large variation in molecular structure gives rise to a very broad spectrum in mechanical behaviour.

 

Typically, without wanting to be exhaustive, three different types can be found in the stress-strain diagram for polymers.

Fig. 1:   The stress-strain diagram for thermosets (curve A), thermoplastics (curve B) and elastomeric polymers (curve C)

 

 

-          Curve A illustrates the behaviour of thermosets: they are generally strong but brittle polymers: they do not constrict and break before they begin to deform plastically.

-          Curve B illustrates the behaviour of a thermoplastic. It is very different: initially the material stretches elastically, until the stress reaches a maximum: the (upper) yield strength. Then it starts to tighten and an area of increasing plastic deformation follows. However, often, unlike metals, the material will not break easily in the constricted area, but over the entire length of the test rod before breaking. The stress may increase again.

-          Curve C: the material behaves almost elastically over the entire area. Here as well, no necking is observed. This rubbery behaviour is characterized by a very high elastic elongation at relatively low stresses. We are talking here about elastomers.

 

Elasticity modulus, tensile strength and ductility (as a percentage of elongation at rupture) are calculated in the same manner for polymers as for metals. Tables C4 and C5 show these properties for a small number of commercial polymers. In the lab there is a similar table from another manufacturer. As you can see, even for one material a large variation in values is possible, depending on molecular structure (chain length, degree of crystallinity,...), production method, additives,....

 

Furthermore, polymers as a class of materials are much less stiff than metals, much less strong, but usually have a much higher fracture strain. For example, the modulus for highly elastic polymeric materials can be as low as 7 MPa, but can be as high as 4 GPa for some very stiff polymers. Modulus values for metals are much higher and range from 48 to 410 GPa. The maximum tensile strength for polymers is in the order of 100 MPa, while for some metal alloys it is 4100 MPa. And while metals can rarely be stretched plastically to more than 100%, some highly e1astical polymers can undergo elongation up to 1000%.

 

 

2.2.        The influence of environmental factors

The mechanical properties of polymers are, much more than with metals, strongly temperature ­dependent in the range around room temperature. Compare the stress-strain diagrams for polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA or Plexiglas) for different temperatures between 4°C and 60°C in Fig. 2. Note that with increasing temperature:

-          the modulus of elasticity decreases,

-          the tensile strength decreases,

-          the ductility or breaking strain increases: at 4°C the material is completely brittle, while there is a large plastic strain at 50°C and 60°C.

 

The influence of the strain rate can also be important. In general, decreasing the strain rate has the same effect as increasing the temperature: the material becomes less stiff and more ductile. The yield stress also decreases.

 

Finally, the chemical nature of the environment influences the mechanical properties. For polymers that absorb water very strongly, (e.g. Polyamide) this effect can be very important: an increasing moisture content will strongly reduce the modulus of elasticity and the yield stress and strongly increase the ductility.

2.3.     The behaviour of semi-crystalline thermoplastic polymers

An understanding of the microscopic deformation mechanisms of polymers is important to understand the macroscopic behaviour of these materials. In this context, two different deformation mechanisms deserve our attention. The first concerns the plastic deformation that occurs in semi-crystalline thermoplastic polymers. The second part explains the behaviour of elastomers and is not discussed here.

 

The stress-strain diagram for a semi-crystalline material that was initially not oriented is shown in Figure 3. The figure also contains schematic images of the test sample at different stages of deformation.

 

                       

 

                        Fig. 3: stress-strain diagram of a semi-crystalline polymer

 

 

With small loads, the material behaves elastically, which means that the strain becomes zero again when the load is removed. The stress in this area is due to the stretching of the chemical bonds within and between the molecules.

 

Then we see a hump in the curve, with higher and lower yield strength. This behaviour is explained as follows. In the crystalline parts of the material the long molecular chains are folded in an ordered pattern (see Fig. 4). As soon as the yield strength is reached, the molecules start to 'unfold' and orient themselves in the direction of force.

 

           

                        Fig. 4: An unstretched polymer crystal consists of folded chains

 

At the upper yield point, a small neck forms within the gauge section of the specimen. Within this neck the molecular chains are oriented, which leads to local reinforcement. As a result, there is an additional resistance to additional deformation in the neck, and the stretching of the test sample continues by extending this necking area over the entire length this section. The phenomenon of molecular orientation goes hand in hand with this constriction.

 

During this extension of the necking area, the tensile force remains more or less constant until all molecular chains have unfolded. The material is stronger in the longitudinal direction, and an increase of the tensile force is necessary to continue the elongation until the material finally breaks.

 

If a semi-crystalline polymer material is stretched until the molecular chains are all oriented in the direction of the force, the material in this direction will be much stronger than the unstretched material.

 

This tensile behaviour is very different from that of ductile metals. In that case reinforcement takes place before the formation of the neck. After a neck is formed, all subsequent deformation is limited to within the neck area and the stress-strain curve bends downwards (see lab text "Tensile test on steel").

 

3.     Experiment

3.1.     Measurement Method

A test rod is clamped between the clamps of a tensile testing machine, and is then pulled at a constant speed until it breaks. A force sensor on the movable crossbeam of the device registers the applied force, while the position of the crossbeam is also registered.

 

A second and more correct method to measure the elongation uses an extensometer. This is a device with 2 movable clamps that are placed on the test rod. These clamps can move freely with the test rod, and their mutual displacement is then measured. This method is more accurate because it only measures the elongation of the part of the rod within the measurement base. However, we will not use them because of the vulnerability of this device.

3.3.     Security aspects

It goes without saying that the general guidelines from the introduction are always followed.

 

Be sure to wear safety goggles, in case a piece of the specimen should jump off in case of breakage. When clamping, the sample is always grasped with pliers, and never with your hands! Make sure that you never put your fingers between the clamps of the puller: danger of crushing!

 

To avoid damaging the tensile testing machine, it is of the utmost importance that the procedure described in the chapter "measurements" is carefully followed!

3.4.4      Determination of the modulus of elasticity.

For thermoplastics, the initial modulus of elasticity is normally calculated from points at 0.05 and 0.25% strain. However, the measured stress-strain diagram shows an irregular behaviour in the beginning, which makes it impossible to determine these points correctly. The initial E-modulus is therefore determined as follows: first remove the points with negative stresses from the table. Then find the beginning of the regular part of the curve. Plot the first (linear) part (the first 10 to 20 points) of this in a new graph. Draw a (linear) trendline and show its equation on the graph. The slope of this line (with the correct units!) is the desired E-modulus. Mention in the result for which strain the graph was drawn. E.g.: E = 1000 MPa at 0.4 to 0.6% strain.

 

For elastomers, one usually mentions the E-modulus at 100% strain. You proceed in a similar way as with thermoplastics, only now you select the part of the graph between 80% and 120% strain. Again, the E-modulus is the slope of the trendline. Note that this value can be different from the initial modulus at very small strains: elastomers often exhibit a nonlinear behaviour in the elastic region.
